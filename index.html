Why use spreadsheets?

Excel isn't just for accountants. It's for everybody, including reporters.

Data is like a tipster. We'll see how you can get story ideas. Who's the highest-paid state employee? How many crimes do cops work at Lenox versus Wal-Mart versus Target?

But you know tipsters. Even a very useful one isn't always perfect. You still need to interview humans who know what's going on. For example, there weren't 107 homicides in Atlanta last year, despite what an otherwise useful data set tells us.

Here are some stories where I guarantee you they used a spreadsheet. The data isn't the whole story, but it was a tipster.

  2. The officer in your rearview mirror: The trooper who writes the most tickets in Ohio
  3. What school districts spend the most money? See rankings here

What kind of file can I open and what's in there?

Excel can open .xls, .xlsx, .csv, .txt, sometimes html tables and some others. I'm not going to go through all those. But keep in mind you can open some exotic filetypes, and you can Google that. "How do I open a .txt file in Excel?" You might need to file > import.

Those files might all be in a .zip file, a compressed file. That's OK, your computer can uncompress it.

Google Sheets is a free alternative to Excel; it's in your Gmail account. It does pretty everything that Excel will in this presentation.

A spreadsheet is just a table or a log. A table with calculating and sorting powers.

Click anywhere in the sheet and go to Data > Sort. Look up in the top right, make sure there's a check beside "My list has headers."

Then tell it you want to sort by salary.

The ports director made nearly $2 million?

Yes and no. Here's the first data reality check. He made $2 million the year he left, once you count salary, bonuses, pensions and other fees. The AJC did the reporting on that.

Clear out all the filters, then just filter by title "coach."

So there's another data thing that fails the logic test. So do a lot of those salaries of $0. Some indeed are board members that don't recieve salary. And some names, for some reason, are entered twice, once for salary and once for travel.

Does it matter that some of the records make no sense? Is this still reliable?

Pro tip: it will take you a long time to scroll. Use a keyboard shortcut to scroll. It will vary by computer and program and version. Mine is crtl + fn + right arrow. Yours may be crtl + down arrow. You may have to Google your shortcut.

Anyway we have 207,572 records. Of which about 2,300 have negative or $0 salaries. And of those, some are understandable, like board members earning $0. And others might be understandable if we asked about them.

Insights & Falsehoods from PivotTables

A PivotTable is going to sum and count things for you. For example, we know can get pretty high at Augusta University. What's its total payroll?

Click anywhere in the table and go to Data > PivotTables

It gives you a cryptic popop. It's asking "what all data" do you want to look at here?

The good news: It's generally by default correct because it assumes "all the data."

The explanation of the good news: "SalaryTravelDataExport2016.csv!$A$1:$F$501733" means "top left to bottom right," as in the picture over there.

So click "OK."

Don't worry if your Excel looks different from mine.

Whatever yours looks like, try clicking & dragging "organization" into "row labels."

Then click and drag the word "salary" into "values."

Then that salary you've dragged down there? Click on the little "i" and change "count by" to "sum."

You're telling it to sum up all the salaries by institution.

And you know what? You can now sort by that "Total" column.

Well gosh, Gwinnett schools have the largest total payroll. Do they have a ton more employees?

Click back into the PivotTable to get all the choices to come back up.

Then click and drag the word "name" into "values."

Then that "name" you've just dragged down there? Click on the little "i" and make sure "count" is selected.

You're telling it to count all the unique names of employees by employer.

It's pretty much a proxy for number of employees. Though yea, there could be more than one "Maggie Lee" somewhere. That's why I say "pretty much."

So that was a fun data set. We got some story leads on high-paid employees.

But eh, the trends weren't so surprising. Pay is pretty high at universities.
